Research Citations Informing This Design

E1 Algorithm Shannon, C.E. (1950). "Programming a Computer for Playing Chess." Philosophical Magazine. — Minimax guarantees optimal play; AI opponent uses this as its core.
E2 Algorithm Knuth, D.E. & Moore, R.W. (1975). "An Analysis of Alpha-Beta Pruning." Artificial Intelligence, 6(4). — Reduces search from O(bd) to O(bd/2). Used in Hard/Impossible modes.
E3 HCI Fitts, P.M. (1954). "The Information Capacity of the Human Motor System." Journal of Experimental Psychology. — Cells are large (110px+) for fast, accurate tapping.
E4 Cognitive Miller, G.A. (1956). "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two." Psychological Review. — Scoreboard limited to 3 values (X wins, O wins, Draws).
E5 HCI Hick, W.E. (1952). "On the Rate of Gain of Information." Quarterly J. of Exp. Psychology. — 9 cells max; clear turn indicator reduces decision time.
E6 A11y W3C (2018). WCAG 2.1 Level AA. — 4.5:1 contrast, full keyboard navigation, ARIA labels, prefers-reduced-motion support.
E7 Perception Wertheimer, M. (1923). Gestalt Laws of Grouping. — Grid lines create cell grouping; spacing separates UI sections.
E8 Design Elliot, A.J. & Maier, M.A. (2014). "Color Psychology." Annual Review of Psychology. — Blue (X) = trust/calm; Red (O) = energy/urgency.
E9 HCI Card, S.K. et al. (1991). "The Model Human Processor." Handbook of HCI. — Feedback under 100ms feels instant; animations tuned accordingly.
E10 UX Nielsen, J. (2006). "Progressive Disclosure." Nielsen Norman Group. — Difficulty selector hidden in PvP mode.
E11 Cognitive Zeigarnik, B. (1927). "On Finished and Unfinished Tasks." — Clear win/draw resolution with prominent restart to prevent incomplete-task tension.
E12 Cognitive Paivio, A. (1971). Imagery and Verbal Processes. — Status bar uses icon + text for dual encoding.
E13 Perception Von Restorff, H. (1933). "Isolation Effect." — Winning line cells highlighted in green, distinct from normal marks.
E14 Perception Weber, E.H. (1834) / Fechner, G.T. (1860). Weber-Fechner Law. — Animation easing uses natural perceptual curves (ease-out-back, ease-out-expo).
E15 Game Theory Nash, J.F. (1950). "Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games." — TTT Nash equilibrium is always a draw with perfect play; Impossible mode demonstrates this.
